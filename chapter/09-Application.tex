\chapter{Application: Neural Theorem Proving}\label{chap:application}

The preceding chapters develop the MMATPG framework in a synthetic setting: finite formula universes, parameterized proof kernels, and controlled dependency structures. This chapter asks whether the framework transfers to real formal mathematics.

We first provide background on formal verification in Lean~4 and the emerging paradigm of neural theorem proving with LLMs (\S\ref{sec:lean-background}). We then describe Agora, a platform that instantiates the MMATPG over real Lean~4 codebases (\S\ref{sec:agora}), with particular attention to the verification pipeline (\S\ref{sec:verified-agora}) and the mapping from real repository structure to MMATPG instances (\S\ref{sec:agora-mapping}). Finally, we evaluate our methodology on the Prime Number Theorem (PNT) formalization against agentic LLM theorem proving baselines (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).


\subsection{Background}\label{sec:lean-background}

\subsubsection{Formal Mathematics and Lean~4}

\paragraph{Proof assistants and the kernel trust model.}
A proof assistant is a software system capable of representing, manipulating, and checking mathematical statements and proofs within a fixed logical foundation. Modern systems implement either set-theoretic foundations (Mizar, Metamath) or dependent type theories (Lean, Rocq, Isabelle/HOL, Agda). The correctness guarantee rests on a key architectural principle, going back to Milner's LCF and de Bruijn's AUTOMATH: isolate a minimal kernel --- a self-contained module implementing only the foundational inference rules --- and require every proof to pass through it. By virtue of its minimality, the kernel can be inspected manually for correctness and serves as a root of trust. Any bug in the elaborator, tactic engine, or automation layer is caught at the kernel boundary: if the kernel accepts a term, the corresponding statement is a theorem of the system, regardless of how the term was produced.

The gap between kernel-level derivations and human-readable mathematical argument is bridged by an elaborator and a high-level vernacular language that supports tactics (proof-producing metaprograms automating repetitive steps), extensible notation, and high-level proof structuring. The process of encoding mathematics in this vernacular is formalization. It is more demanding than informal argumentation --- every detail, edge case, and side condition must be explicitly accounted for --- but produces machine-checkable certificates of correctness.

\paragraph{Lean~4 and Mathlib.}
Lean~4, whose first stable release appeared in 2023, is distinguished from earlier proof assistants by radical extensibility: nearly all of Lean's implementation (elaborator, compiler, tactic engine) is written in Lean itself, making it straightforward to invoke and modify language internals from user code. This bootstrapped design allows tactics and automation to be written as ordinary Lean programs, reusing production-strength data structures and algorithms without crossing language boundaries.

The primary mathematical library for Lean~4 is Mathlib, a monolithic, collaboratively maintained repository in which every mathematical concept is defined once, in a globally agreed-upon way. As of 2025, Mathlib contains approximately 1.9 million lines of Lean code, growing at roughly 500 thousand lines per year, and covers a broad sweep of modern mathematics: from basic algebra and topology to condensed mathematics, perfectoid spaces, and the independence of the continuum hypothesis. Mathlib's scale and coherence make it the primary substrate for large-scale formalization projects and for training and evaluating neural theorem-proving systems.

\paragraph{The coordination structure of formalization.}
A key property of Lean projects (and of Mathlib in particular) is that proofs are interdependent. Each declaration's proof may invoke hundreds of prior lemmas, and those lemmas in turn depend on others. This induces a declaration-level directed acyclic graph over the entire library. A consequence of this structure is that formalization is inherently collaborative and cumulative: building a proof of a deep theorem requires not just a clever top-level argument, but the coordinated development of supporting infrastructure that may not yet exist. This is precisely the coordination problem that the MMATPG framework addresses.

Formal verification also enables a mode of decentralized, trustless collaboration that is unusual in mathematics. Because correctness is guaranteed by the kernel rather than by trust in the author, contributors with no prior relationship---and no established reputation in the community---can make verifiable contributions to shared projects. This has enabled large-scale distributed formalization efforts (e.g. the Equational Theories project) that would be difficult to organize under traditional mathematical norms.



\subsubsection{Neural Theorem Proving with LLMs}

\paragraph{Tactic-level proof search.}
Early neural theorem provers operate at the level of individual tactic steps. Given a proof state (the current goal and local hypotheses), a model generates a tactic, Lean evaluates it, and the resulting new proof state is fed back. This search-based paradigm --- exemplified by GPT-$f$ \citep{polu2020generative}, PACT \citep{han2021proof}, and ReProver \citep{yang2023leandojo} --- treats each proof obligation as an isolated tree search problem. The model learns a heuristic policy over proof states, typically trained on corpora of human-written proofs extracted from Mathlib or other libraries. Best-first search or Monte Carlo tree search is then used to find a complete proof.

Tactic-level approaches have demonstrated strong performance on benchmarks of isolated lemmas (MiniF2F \citep{zheng2021minif2f}, FIMO \citep{liu2023fimo}) but face a fundamental limitation: they operate in a fixed context, unaware of the broader project structure. They cannot decide which lemma to attempt, in what order, or how their work relates to other obligations. Each proof is attacked as if it existed in isolation.

\paragraph{Agentic LLM provers.}
More recent systems give LLMs broader autonomy over the formalization process. Rather than generating a single tactic step, an agentic prover can read source files, inspect goal states, write multi-step proofs, submit them for type-checking, interpret error messages, revise, and iterate. Aristotle Agent, OpenGauss, and systems based on Claude Code with Lean LSP access exemplify this paradigm. These agents operate over entire Lean projects, not individual proof states, and can in principle navigate dependency structures by deciding which lemmas to prove in which order.

The MiniCTX benchmark \citep{hu2024minictx} was developed specifically to evaluate this capability: unlike isolated-lemma benchmarks, MiniCTX presents each proof obligation within its full project context, requiring the prover to understand imports, existing definitions, and inter-declaration dependencies. This is a substantially harder setting, and current SOTA systems achieve only partial coverage even on accessible repositories.

\paragraph{The coordination gap.}
Despite operating at the project level, existing multi-agent agentic provers treat coordination as an afterthought. When multiple agents operate on the same project, they typically receive disjoint target assignments from a central scheduler and interact only through a shared repository. There is no structured mechanism for: (i) signaling which intermediate results would be valuable to other agents, (ii) incentivizing the publication of by-product lemmas that happen to be solved along the way, or (iii) sequencing proof attempts to respect and exploit the dependency structure. The result is duplicated effort, missed dependency-unlocking opportunities, and systematic underinvestment in shared infrastructure---precisely the market failure that the MMATPG framework is designed to address.

\paragraph{Formal mathematics as an allocation problem.}
The framing of this thesis is that theorem proving at scale is not primarily a search problem (finding a proof of a given statement) but an allocation problem (deciding which agents should attempt which obligations in which order, and how to incentivize the sharing of intermediate results). The search problem---generating a valid proof given a target---is handled by the LLM subagent and is outside the scope of the coordination framework. The allocation problem is what the MMATPG formalizes and what the strategy module learns to solve.


\subsection{The Agora Platform}\label{sec:agora}

Agora is an infrastructure platform that instantiates the MMATPG for real Lean~4 projects. It provides library mechanics (git-backed shared and private libraries with verified publication), market mechanics (collateralized target-conditioned contracts with automated settlement), and an agent interface (MCP-based tool access for LLM agents).

\subsubsection{Architecture}\label{sec:agora-arch}

\input{figures/agora_diagram}



The Agora runtime consists of three backend services and an external orchestration harness.

\paragraph{Library Mechanics.}
The service responsible for all Lean interaction. It maintains an LRU cache of project repositories cloned from GitHub forks onto Agora-specific branches, managing all project initialization, building, and target extraction. Moreover, the library mechanics module invokes VerifiedAgora to validate or reject attempted contributions and merge them to the shared branch. A dependency-aware request scheduler serializes concurrent contributions to the same project, ensuring that non-ephemeral completions correctly unblock downstream module builds.

\paragraph{Market Mechanics.}
The financial core, owning wallets (cash balances, private assets, posted offers), target statuses, and the public offer book. It enforces a (i) money conservation invariant: the net asset diff of each transaction, after simplifying all target-conditioned assets against current resolution data, must have zero net coefficient; and a (ii) liquidity invariant: each wallet's minimum value (cash plus lower bounds of held assets) must remain non-negative. When a target is resolved, Market Mechanics records the proof time and proving agent, simplifies all referencing assets and offers, and converts any fully determined assets into wallet balance.

\paragraph{Backend Interface.}
The public API gateway coordinating the two mechanics services. It handles agent authentication (API keys with bcrypt hashing, or Supabase JWTs), synchronizes target metadata between library mechanics and market mechanics, broadcasts activity events over WebSocket (contributions, target updates, trades), and exposes a MCP server. The backend delegates all Lean validation to library mechanics and all financial invariant enforcement to market mechanics.

\paragraph{Agent Orchestrator.}
A separate Claude Agent SDK-based harness that provisions agent containers, manages their lifecycle, and provides a persistent loop for LLM interaction. Namely, each agent container runs an LLM with access to MCP-based library tools (e.g. list projects, submit contributions, etc.); market tools (e.g. create/take/cancel offers, transfer assets, etc.); Lean LSP tools (e.g. goal state, hover info, etc.); and standard filesystem tools.

The agent runtime maintains structured state (active project, target, wallet, build status, market positions), and includes recovery hints for common failure modes (edit mismatches, Lean compilation errors, path resolution issues). After each LLM response, the harness reengages with updated status, recent market events, and action suggestions. Auto-compaction summarizes long conversations into a memory file to prevent context overflow.


\subsubsection{Verification}\label{sec:verified-agora}

VerifiedAgora is a Lean~4 library that provides the trusted verification layer.

\paragraph{Target attribute.}
A Lean tag attribute \texttt{@[target]} marks declarations as Agora proof obligations. Contracts in the market are tied to these tagged declarations.

\paragraph{Axiom policy.}
Non-target declarations may only transitively depend on three foundational axioms: \texttt{propext}, \texttt{Quot.sound}, and \texttt{Classical.choice}. Target declarations may additionally depend on \texttt{sorryAx} while unresolved, but a \emph{solved} target must be sorry-free and axiom-clean. Axiom usage is collected via batched recursive traversal of declaration types and values, with cycle protection and cross-declaration caching.

\paragraph{Contribution validation.}
When an agent submits Lean content, VerifiedAgora: (i) compiles the submission; (ii) extracts and matches declarations against the existing target descriptor, checking type/kind/attribute/instance compatibility and axiom policy; (iii) produces an automerged file where valid solved targets replace sorry'd versions, invalid submissions revert to originals, and new non-target declarations are included; (iv) writes the automerged file and runs \texttt{lake build}; if the build fails, the original file is restored. This conservative pipeline ensures agents cannot break the repository.



\subsubsection{Inference-Time MMATPG Mapping}\label{sec:inference-mapping}

We now define how each MMATPG component is instantiated at inference time in an online fashion, when real LLM agents operate within the deployed Agora scaffold.

\paragraph{Time Discretization}\label{sec:time-discretization}

The MMATPG is a discrete-time game with simultaneous actions. In the real Lean setting, agents operate asynchronously in continuous wall-clock time: proof attempts take variable durations, market actions are near-instantaneous, and agents do not move in lockstep.

We discretize time into decision epochs indexed by $t \in \mathbb{N}$, where each epoch corresponds to one LLM interaction step (one prompt $\to$ response cycle). Within a single epoch, an agent may take exactly one high-level action: initiate a proof attempt, publish a result, submit a query, or take a market action. Proof attempts are durative: initiating a proof at epoch $t$ with budget $\tau$ means the strategic agent is occupied (forced to take $\bot$) for the next $\tau$ epochs while the prover subagent it iterates on the proof. Publication and market actions are instantaneous (consume one epoch).

This discretization has two consequences. First, the ``budget'' $\tau$ of a proof attempt is measured in LLM interaction steps, not wall-clock seconds or tokens. This normalizes across agents with different inference speeds. Second, because agents operate asynchronously, the simultaneous-action semantics of the MMATPG are approximated: in practice, noop's are synthetically inserted [\textbf{todo explain how/when/why?}]

\begin{remark}
This design choice reflects a tradeoff between fidelity and tractability. A fully asynchronous, continuous-time model would be more realistic but significantly more complex to analyze and implement. Additionally, the lack to distinction between faster, smaller models and slower, larger models is a limitation to the practical measurement of heterogeneous agent capabilities. Future work could explore more nuanced time models that account for these differences, such as token-based budgets or continuous frameworks.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Component Mapping}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Formulas and sequents: Lean declarations of type \texttt{Prop} serve as formulas. Each \texttt{@[target]}-tagged declaration is a target formula. The sequent $[\Gamma \proves \phi]$ is identified with the target declaration, since the antecedent $\Gamma$ is determined by the import graph and the current shared branch state.
    \item Libraries: The shared Git branch is $\mathcal{L}^0_t$. Each agent's local workspace is $\mathcal{L}^\alpha_t$. The inclusion invariant $\mathcal{L}^0_t \subseteq \mathcal{L}^\alpha_t$ holds because copies are initialized from the shared volume and updated via git pull after each public commit.
    \item Resolution and dependencies: A target is resolved when it passes VerifiedAgora's full pipeline. Dependencies $\delta^*(s)$ are extracted post-hoc by the batched axiom collector.
    \item Publication: \texttt{write\_contribution} $\mapsto$ commit to shared branch $\mapsto$ Market Mechanics settlement.
    
    \item Market: \texttt{PriceySatisfiedByAsset(target, stop\_time, price)} implements $\chi^+ = (s, T, \ell)$ with $\ell = \mathrm{price}$. The bounty agent seeds long offers on all open targets at $\ell = 0.1$.
    
    \item Proof kernel: The LLM agent reads the target, inspects the goal state via LSP, writes a tactic proof, and submits via \texttt{write\_contribution}. Lean's type-checker and VerifiedAgora serve as the binary oracle: $(1, \delta^*(s))$ on success, $(0, \bot)$ on failure. The processing time $T^\alpha_s$ is the number of decision epochs consumed by the attempt.
    \item Conjecture kernel: The LLM proposes new intermediate declarations by writing them into the Lean file. If the declaration type-checks and is accepted as a new non-target declaration, it becomes concrete in the agent's private library and may be published. The proposal distribution is induced by the LLM's prior over useful statements conditioned on proof context.
    \item Query model: Given a target, the LLM is prompted to estimate the target's likelihood of success and expected duration to proof, based on its proof history and current library state. These estimates are passed to the strategy module as $(\hat{p}, \hat{\tau})$.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Synthetic Training Data Generation}\label{sec:training}

Training the strategy module on real Lean is impractical: each proof attempt requires minutes of LLM inference and Lean compilation, and the target set is fixed across episodes. Instead, we train on synthetic MMATPG instances whose structural statistics are calibrated to real Lean repositories, with the LLM subagent replaced by a tractable parameterized kernel. This section describes the calibration pipeline.


\subsubsection{Offline MMATPG Extraction}\label{sec:dep-extraction}


\paragraph{Utility Graph Extraction}\label{sec:util-extraction}

Given a complete target Lean repository, we extract the declaration-level dependency DAG, estimate per-target difficulty, and compute utility weights. Namely, we metaprogrammatically invoke Lean's internal dependency extraction tools, by parsing the built \texttt{.olean} cache, which stores \texttt{Environment}-level data on declarations, their types, values, attributes, and dependencies. This is done recursively over all modules in the target project, excluding infrastructure namespaces (\texttt{Lean}, \texttt{Init}, \texttt{Std}, etc.). The result is a dependency index $\{d \mapsto [d_1, \ldots, d_k]\}$.

From the dependency index, we compute per-declaration dependency frequency (how often a declaration appears as a dependency of others) and relative frequency (normalized by total dependency count). For each target $\phi$, the rarity score is:
\[
\mathrm{rarity}(\phi) = \sum_{p \in \mathrm{deps}(\phi)} \frac{1}{\mathrm{rel\_freq}(p)}.
\]
Targets depending on rare prerequisites receive higher rarity. We quantile-match these scores against a Mathlib-calibrated percentile table (derived from empirical Mathlib dependency statistics) and apply $\log(1+\cdot)$ smoothing to compress outliers.

Normalized rarity maps to difficulty:
\[
d(\phi) = 0.05 + 0.90 \cdot \mathrm{normalized\_rarity}(\phi) \in [0.05, 0.95].
\]

Note for evaluations, we bucket targets into difficulty tiers based on $d(\phi)$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Easy} ($d(\phi) < 0.40$): Targets with few or no sorry'd dependencies, whose prerequisites are mostly standard lemmas (high relative frequency). 
    % These are primarily algebraic manipulations, norm estimates, or direct applications of existing Mathlib infrastructure. An LLM agent can in principle attempt them immediately, without first resolving other targets. Examples from the PNT project: basic properties of the von Mangoldt function, elementary bounds on divisor sums.
 
    \item \textbf{Medium} ($0.40 \le d(\phi) < 0.70$, $n=41$): Targets with a mix of standard and specialized prerequisites, or which depend on one to three other sorry'd targets. These require some domain-specific reasoning, but the dependency chain is shallow enough that a well-coordinated agent pair can resolve them in sequence.
    %  Examples: Mertens' theorems, partial summation estimates, basic properties of the Riemann zeta function.
 
    \item \textbf{Hard} ($d(\phi) \ge 0.70$, $n=12$): Targets deep in the dependency DAG, with multiple unresolved upstream sorry's and prerequisites that are themselves rare (low relative frequency), require a chain of prerequisite proofs to be resolved first.
\end{itemize}
 



Moreover, for each ordered pair $(\psi, \phi)$, we compute $k$-hop dependency overlap ($k=2$, decay $0.3$). Direct dependencies: $w(\psi, \phi) = 1.0$. Second-hop overlap: weighted by decay and normalized by target dependency mass. Non-overlapping pairs: floored at $0.01$. The coverage formula is:
\[
w(\psi, \phi) = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(\frac{\sum_x \mathrm{wt}(x) \cdot \min\big(\mathrm{src\_deps}(x),\, \mathrm{tgt\_deps}(x)\big)}{\sum_x \mathrm{wt}(x) \cdot \mathrm{tgt\_deps}(x)},\; 0.01,\; 0.99\right),
\]
overridden to $1.0$ for hard dependencies. This produces a dense utility graph reflecting the structural relationships of the real codebase. This will serve as a distributional prior for sampling structurally similar synthetic instances for training the strategy module.


\paragraph{Kernel Calibration}\label{sec:kernel-calibration}

Unlike at inference-time, during the synthetic data training stage the LLM subagent is replaced by parameterized probability kernels. For example, the synthetic proof kernel from Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments} has parameters $(\rho_0^\alpha, \rho_1^\alpha, \lambda_{\mathrm{diff}}, \kappa)$. We calibrate these heuristically as tunable hyperparameters.

% We note this is a limitation of our evaluation methodology: this arbitrary hyperparameter tuning does not guarantee that the synthetic kernel is a faithful model of the subagent's reasoning capabilities, and thus the strategy module trained on this kernel may learn coordination strategies that are not effective for real LLM agents. Future work could explore more rigorous calibration methodologies, such as collecting detailed performance data from the LLM agents on a representative subset of targets, and fitting the kernel parameters to minimize the discrepancy between the kernel's predicted success rates and the observed success rates across different difficulty tiers and budget levels.


\subsubsection{Synthetic DAG Generation}\label{sec:synthetic-dag}

Given the extracted statistics, we generate synthetic training datasets of MMATPG environments with fixed kernel hyperparameters and sampled utility graphs, generated with the aforementioned prior\ref{sec:util-extraction} such that the sampled environments are structurally similar to the target repository.

\paragraph{Generator design.}


The \texttt{SyntheticDAG} generator produces layered, clustered dependency graphs that preserve the structural statistics of the real repository while enabling fast, stateless resampling. The generator is controlled by the following hyperparameters, each of which corresponds to a measurable property of the target codebase.
 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Layer count ($n_{\mathrm{layers}}$): The number of topological layers in the DAG. Edges are only permitted from higher to lower layers, enforcing acyclicity by construction. A node's layer governs both which other nodes it can depend on and how specialized its dependency profile is (deeper layers have stronger self-affinity). Set to match the ratio of the repository's DAG depth to its average chain length.
 
    \item Cluster count ($n_{\mathrm{clusters}}$): The number of topic clusters, each receiving a distinct Dirichlet-sampled dependency profile over source clusters. Clusters correspond loosely to mathematical subdisciplines (e.g., analytic number theory and complex analysis in the PNT project). Cross-cluster dependencies model the connections between subdisciplines.
 
    \item Self-affinity ($\gamma_{\mathrm{self}} \ge 0$): A bonus added to the diagonal of each cluster's dependency profile before normalization. Higher values produce more topic-local dependency graphs, where theorems depend predominantly on results within the same subdiscipline. Lower values produce more cross-cutting dependencies.
 
    \item Profile concentration ($c_{\mathrm{profile}} > 0$): The concentration parameter of the Dirichlet distribution from which each cluster's dependency profile is sampled. Lower values produce sparse profiles (each cluster depends heavily on a few source clusters); higher values produce more diffuse, uniform profiles. Set to match the empirical evenness of cross-cluster dependencies in the target repository.
 
    \item Foundation nodes ($n_{\mathrm{found}}$, $p_{\mathrm{found}}$): The number of lowest-layer nodes designated as universal prerequisites (analogous to common Mathlib imports like basic arithmetic or topological preliminaries), and the probability that any non-foundation node includes one to three foundation dependencies. These create global overlap across otherwise separate graph regions.
 
    \item In-degree distribution ($[\tau_{\min}, \tau_{\max}]$, $\mu_{\mathrm{deg}}$): The range and mean of the clipped exponential distribution from which each node's dependency count is sampled. This controls the overall sparsity of the DAG and is set to match the target repository's empirical degree distribution.
\end{itemize}
 
The rarity-to-difficulty mapping is shared between the real extraction pipeline and the synthetic generator: once synthetic nodes are assigned rarity scores (computed from the generated DAG's dependency frequencies using the same formula as in \S\ref{sec:dep-extraction}), they are quantile-matched against the same Mathlib-calibrated percentile table and mapped to $[0.05, 0.95]$. This ensures that the difficulty distribution of synthetic instances statistically matches that of the real repository, even though the specific theorems and their mathematical content differ.


% Training proceeds over seven curriculum levels ($n \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256\}$ theorem pairs), progressively shifting probability mass toward larger instances. We fix $8$ agents for all instances. Before each rollout, the environment is regenerated, sampling a fresh graph from the calibrated distribution. This ensures the policy sees many structurally similar but non-identical instances, promoting generalization over memorization.

\subsubsection{Training-to-Inference Gap}\label{sec:gap}

The key gap between training and inference is that the parameterized kernels are stationary and Markovian, while the LLM agent is neither: its proof success probability depends on its entire interaction history, and its conjecture proposals depend on mathematical reasoning not captured by the utility-graph heuristic.

We mitigate this through three mechanisms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Conservative calibration.} Kernel parameters are fitted to observed (not idealized) LLM success rates, so the strategy module does not learn to rely on proof capabilities the LLM does not possess.
    \item \textbf{Kernel noise.} Stochastic noise in the proof/conjecture/query kernels prevents the strategy module from overfitting to deterministic oracle behavior. The trained module must be robust to outcome variance.
    \item \textbf{Prompt-based deployment.} At inference time, the strategy module's recommendations are injected into the LLM's prompt, not enforced as hard constraints. The LLM can deviate from recommendations (and does so on $\sim\!15\%$ of steps), adapting the strategy to its own proof state. This soft coupling absorbs training-to-inference distributional shift.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Strategy Module Training}\label{sec:strategy-training}

The strategy module is the GPT actor from Chapter~\ref{chap:marl}, trained on PNT-calibrated synthetic instances (\S\ref{sec:training}). Training uses the full MARL pipeline (PPO + GAE + entropy annealing + population-based training + five-level curriculum). The trained module is applied \emph{zero-shot} to the real Lean project.

At deployment, the strategy module observes the Agora scaffold state (embedded as an action-epoch-discretized MMATPG instance) and outputs high-level actions, serialized into the LLM agent's prompt at each decision epoch. The LLM retains full autonomy over proof/conjecture construction.


\subsubsection{Systems Under Comparison}\label{sec:systems}

We compare the following Agora-based neural theorem proving systems:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{B1.}] \textbf{Agora Scaffold ($8$ agents).} LLM agents on the Agora platform with no strategy module. The bounty agent seeds offers on all targets. Structural advantages over A2: verified publication, market bounties, standardized MCP interface.
    \item[\textbf{C1.}] \textbf{Agora + Strategy ($8$ agents).} Same scaffold as B1, with the MARL-trained strategy module injecting recommendations into each agent's prompt. In practice, agents follow recommendations on $\sim\!85\%$ of decision epochs.
\end{itemize}

Against the following agentic LLM baselines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{A1.}] \textbf{Claude Code ($1$ agent).} A single Claude instance with Lean LSP access. Serial processing, no coordination.
    \item[\textbf{A2.}] \textbf{Claude Code Teams ($8$ agents).} A single Claude Code Teams instance with Lean LSP access specified to have 8 parallel agents.
    \item[\textbf{A3.}] \textbf{Aristotle Agent.} State-of-the-art agentic Lean prover, evaluated as a black box.
    \item[\textbf{A4.}] \textbf{OpenGauss.} Another SOTA system, same evaluation protocol.
\end{itemize}


\begin{remark}[Strategy module as controller]
The action-space mapping is: $\mathrm{Prove}(\phi) \mapsto$ direct the LLM to attempt target $\phi$; $\mathrm{Pub}(\phi) \mapsto$ call \texttt{write\_contribution}; $\mathrm{Qry}(\phi) \mapsto$ prompt for difficulty estimate; market actions $\mapsto$ Agora market MCP tools. The LLM may deviate because the strategy module's output is a prompt injection, not a hard constraint.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[LLM subroutines]
Future work may study a variant where the strategy module triggers separate LLM subroutines with specialized prompts (e.g., a ``proof attempt'' subroutine), more closely mirroring the theoretical MMAPTG separation of strategy and proof kernel.
\end{remark}


\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol}\label{sec:protocol}

All systems receive the same fixed total wall-clock time budget $B$. Moreover, we focus on the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathrm{Resolve\%}$: fraction of $\Phi^*$ with sorry-free, VerifiedAgora-certified proofs (overall and by tier).
    \item $\mathrm{WallTime}_{x\%}$: wall-clock time to reach $x\%$ resolution.
    \item $\mathrm{Duplication\%}$: fraction of proof attempts targeting a declaration already solved by another agent.
    \item $\mathrm{DepUnlock}$: count of dependency-unlock events.
    \item $\mathrm{Discovery}$: number of new (non-target) declarations introduced and used in downstream proofs.
\end{itemize}

The core evaluation is the standard sorry-filling task: given the full PNT repository with all targets (MiniCTX-v2) replaced by sorry (and equally economically bountied, as necessary), systems must resolve as many targets as possible within the time budget. This tests the overall effectiveness of the coordination mechanisms in guiding agents to efficiently allocate their proof efforts.


We additionally study a qualitative example trajectory of this evaluation.

Systems must now conjecture and prove the missing intermediate results before they can resolve the original targets. This tests the conjecture kernel and dependency-chain navigation in a setting where the dependency structure must be partially reconstructed.


\subsubsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The arbitrary kernel hyperparameter tuning does not guarantee that the synthetic kernel is a faithful model of the subagent’s reasoning capabilities, and thus the strategy module trained on this kernel may learn coordination strategies that are not effective for real LLM agents. Future work could explore more rigorous calibration methodologies, such as collecting detailed performance data from the LLM agents on a representative subset of targets, and fitting the kernel parameters to minimize the discrepancy between the kernel’s predicted success rates and the observed success rates across different difficulty tiers and budget levels.
    \item The strategy module was trained on synthetic instances and applied zero-shot. Domain shift may reduce effectiveness; fine-tuning on real Lean data is a natural next step.
    \item Comparisons to A3/A4 are against public configurations not specifically tuned for MiniCTX PNT.
    \item Compute normalization is approximate: LLM API calls, Lean kernel checks, and strategy-module inference have different costs. Decision-epoch discretization (\S\ref{sec:time-discretization}) is a simplification of the true asynchronous setting.
    \item The Agora market is simplified relative to full MMATPG theory: contract types are restricted to target declarations, deadlines are coarsened, and the bounty agent uses fixed prices.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Qualitative Results: A Case Study in Distributed Proof Development}\label{sec:qualitative}
 
To illustrate how the Agora scaffold distributes and sequences work across agents, we trace the resolution of a single dependency chain in the PNT formalization. The terminal target is \texttt{SmallSquareInRectangle}, a medium-difficulty lemma stating that if a complex point $p$ lies in the interior of a rectangle, then for all sufficiently small $c > 0$, the axis-aligned square of side $2c$ centered at $p$ is contained in the rectangle.
 
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Target lemma \texttt{SmallSquareInRectangle} (lines 826--835).},label={lst:target}]
lemma SmallSquareInRectangle {z w p : ℂ} (pInRectInterior : Rectangle z w ∈ nhds p) :
    ∀ᶠ (c : ℝ) in 𝓝[>]0, Square p c ⊆ Rectangle z w := by
      obtain ⟨ε, hε_pos, hε⟩ : ∃ ε > 0, ∀ c : ℝ, 0 < c → c < ε → Square p c ⊆ Rectangle z w := by
        obtain ⟨ε, hε⟩ : ∃ ε > 0, Square p ε ⊆ z.Rectangle w := by
          have := Complex.nhds_hasBasis_square p;
          exact this.mem_iff.mp pInRectInterior;
        exact ⟨ ε, hε.1, fun c hc₁ hc₂ =>
          Set.Subset.trans ( square_subset_square ( by linarith ) ( by linarith ) ) hε.2 ⟩;
      filter_upwards [ Ioo_mem_nhdsGT hε_pos ] with c hc using hε c hc.1 hc.2
\end{lstlisting}
 
The proof invokes two non-trivial lemmas: \texttt{Complex.nhds\_hasBasis\_square} (line 832), which states that squares centered at $p$ form a neighborhood basis, and \texttt{square\_subset\_square} (line 834), which states that squares are monotone in their side length. At the outset of the run, both of these were themselves \texttt{sorry}'d targets, and each had its own upstream sorry'd prerequisites. The full dependency chain is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:case-study-chain}.

\paragraph{Bottom of the chain: conjectured helper lemmas.}
At the start of the run, the chain's roots were not present in the Lean file at all. The two trivial helpers \texttt{left\_mem\_rect} and \texttt{right\_mem\_rect}---asserting that the corner points of a rectangle lie within it---were conjectured by agent $\alpha_2$ midway through its attempt to prove \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff}. Upon introspecting the proof goal and identifying that the forward direction of the iff required concrete witnesses, $\alpha_2$ issued a $\mathrm{Conj}(\phi)$ action whose proposal distribution (conditioned on the current proof context) placed high mass on exactly these two helper statements. The conjectured lemmas were trivial and resolved in the same epoch:
 
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Conjectured leaf lemmas, introduced and resolved by $\alpha_2$ at epoch $t\!\approx\!1500\,\mathrm{s}$ (lines 499--509).},label={lst:leaves}]
lemma left_mem_rect (z w : ℂ) : z ∈ Rectangle z w := by
  apply And.intro (by norm_num) (by norm_num)
 
lemma right_mem_rect (z w : ℂ) : w ∈ Rectangle z w := by
  apply And.intro;
  · simp [Set.mem_Icc];
  · simp [Set.mem_Icc]
\end{lstlisting}
 
Once these helpers succeeded and they were committed to the shared branch, the backend broadcast an event, meaning all other agents received updated state on their next reengagement; their subsequent proof attempts could now invoke these lemmas as ordinary import references.

\paragraph{The first target: \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff}.}
Agent $\alpha_2$, having conjectured and resolved the helpers, immediately proceeded to the first bona fide target in the chain. \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff} states that $\texttt{Rectangle}\ z'\ w' \subseteq \texttt{Rectangle}\ z\ w$ if and only if both endpoints of the inner rectangle lie in the outer:
 
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={\texttt{rect\_subset\_iff}, resolved by $\alpha_2$ (lines 511--520).},label={lst:rectsubsetiff}]
lemma rect_subset_iff {z w z' w' : ℂ} :
    Rectangle z' w' ⊆ Rectangle z w ↔ z' ∈ Rectangle z w ∧ w' ∈ Rectangle z w := by
      refine' ⟨ fun h => ⟨ _, _ ⟩, fun h => _ ⟩;
      · exact h ( left_mem_rect _ _ );
      · apply h; exact right_mem_rect z' w';
      · unfold Rectangle at *;
        simp_all +decide [ Set.subset_def, Complex.mem_reProdIm ];
        unfold Set.uIcc at *;
        grind
\end{lstlisting}
 
The forward direction uses \texttt{left\_mem\_rect} and \texttt{right\_mem\_rect} directly (lines 514 and 516); the reverse direction is discharged by \texttt{grind} after unfolding definitions. $\alpha_2$ published this result at approximately epoch $t\!\approx\!2100\,\mathrm{s}$.
 
\paragraph{Parallel work on the other branch.}
While $\alpha_2$ was working at the bottom of the \texttt{square\_subset\_square} branch, agent $\alpha_3$ was simultaneously making progress on the other direct parent of \texttt{SmallSquareInRectangle}---the neighborhood basis lemma \texttt{Complex.nhds\_hasBasis\_square}. This target's upstream dependencies (\texttt{Square\_apply}, \texttt{rectangle\_mem\_nhds\_iff}) were already resolved in Mathlib, so $\alpha_3$ could attack it directly without waiting for any publication events. This proof is the technically hardest in the chain: it requires showing both that every standard neighborhood contains some square and that every square is itself a neighborhood.
 
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={\texttt{Complex.nhds\_hasBasis\_square}, resolved by $\alpha_3$ (lines 787--801). Longest and most technically demanding proof in the chain.},label={lst:nhdsbasis}]
theorem Complex.nhds_hasBasis_square (p : ℂ) : (𝓝 p).HasBasis (0 < ·) (Square p ·) := by
  refine' ⟨ fun s => ⟨ fun hs => _, fun hs => _ ⟩ ⟩;
  · rw [ Metric.mem_nhds_iff ] at hs;
    have h_square_ball : ∀ ε > 0, Square p (ε / 2) ⊆ Metric.ball p ε := by
      intro ε hε;
      intro x hx; rw [ Square_apply p ( half_pos hε ) ] at hx;
      simp_all +decide [ Complex.dist_eq, Complex.normSq ];
      exact Real.sqrt_lt' hε |>.2
        ( by norm_num [ Complex.normSq ] ; nlinarith [ hx.1.1, hx.1.2, hx.2.1, hx.2.2 ] );
    exact ⟨ hs.choose / 2, half_pos hs.choose_spec.1,
      Set.Subset.trans ( h_square_ball _ hs.choose_spec.1 ) hs.choose_spec.2 ⟩;
  · unfold Square at hs;
    obtain ⟨ i, hi, hi' ⟩ := hs;
    refine' Filter.mem_of_superset _ hi';
    rw [ rectangle_mem_nhds_iff ];
    norm_num [ Set.uIoo ];
    exact ⟨
      ⟨ by cases max_cases (-i + p.re) (i + p.re) <;>
             cases min_cases (-i + p.re) (i + p.re) <;> linarith,
        by cases max_cases (-i + p.re) (i + p.re) <;>
             cases min_cases (-i + p.re) (i + p.re) <;> linarith ⟩,
      ⟨ by cases max_cases (-i + p.im) (i + p.im) <;>
             cases min_cases (-i + p.im) (i + p.im) <;> linarith,
        by cases max_cases (-i + p.im) (i + p.im) <;>
             cases min_cases (-i + p.im) (i + p.im) <;> linarith ⟩⟩
\end{lstlisting}
 
$\alpha_3$ published this result at epoch $t\!\approx\!2550\,\mathrm{s}$, slightly after $\alpha_2$'s \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff} publication.
 
\paragraph{Parallel conjecturing and the utility branch.}
Meanwhile, agent $\alpha_1$ had been assigned \texttt{square\_subset\_square} by the strategy module. Upon inspecting this goal, $\alpha_1$ determined that the natural proof required an intermediate lemma relating rectangle inclusion to coordinate-wise inequalities in a stronger form than \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff}. $\alpha_1$ conjectured \texttt{RectSubRect'}, a useful but non-target generalization expressing rectangle inclusion directly in terms of componentwise real/imaginary inequalities:
 
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={\texttt{RectSubRect'}, conjectured and resolved by $\alpha_1$ (lines 546--558), immediately after $\alpha_2$'s \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff} publication.},label={lst:rectsubrectp}]
lemma RectSubRect' {z₀ z₁ z₂ z₃ : ℂ} (x₀_le_x₁ : z₀.re ≤ z₁.re) (x₁_le_x₂ : z₁.re ≤ z₂.re)
    (x₂_le_x₃ : z₂.re ≤ z₃.re) (y₀_le_y₁ : z₀.im ≤ z₁.im) (y₁_le_y₂ : z₁.im ≤ z₂.im)
    (y₂_le_y₃ : z₂.im ≤ z₃.im) :
    Rectangle z₁ z₂ ⊆ Rectangle z₀ z₃ := by
      apply rect_subset_iff.mpr;
      apply And.intro;
      · apply And.intro;
        · exact Set.mem_uIcc_of_le ( by linarith ) ( by linarith );
        · exact Set.mem_uIcc_of_le y₀_le_y₁ ( by linarith );
      · constructor <;>
          [ exact Set.mem_uIcc_of_le ( by linarith ) ( by linarith )
          ; exact Set.mem_uIcc_of_le ( by linarith ) ( by linarith ) ]
\end{lstlisting}
 
Notice that this proof directly invokes \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff.mpr} at line 551---a reference that was only valid after $\alpha_2$'s publication at $t\!\approx\!2100\,\mathrm{s}$. The dependency-unlock event triggered a reengagement of $\alpha_1$, whose strategy-module recommendation was updated to proceed with the stalled \texttt{RectSubRect'} attempt. $\alpha_1$ published \texttt{RectSubRect'} at $t\!\approx\!2300\,\mathrm{s}$.
 
\paragraph{Closing the square branch.}
With \texttt{RectSubRect'} now public, $\alpha_1$ resolved \texttt{square\_subset\_square} almost immediately:
 
\begin{lstlisting}[caption={\texttt{square\_subset\_square}, resolved by $\alpha_1$ at $t\!\approx\!2400\,\mathrm{s}$ (lines 819--824).},label={lst:squaresquare}]
lemma square_subset_square {p : ℂ} {c₁ c₂ : ℝ} (hc₁ : 0 < c₁) (hc : c₁ ≤ c₂) :
    Square p c₁ ⊆ Square p c₂ := by
      apply RectSubRect';
      all_goals norm_num [ Complex.ext_iff ] at *; linarith
\end{lstlisting}
 
The proof is a two-line invocation of the conjectured lemma \texttt{RectSubRect'} (line 822) followed by a uniform \texttt{linarith} closer. This is a striking example of what the conjecture action enables: $\alpha_1$ produced a four-line final proof because it had earlier invested effort in creating the right auxiliary abstraction. A system without a conjecture primitive would have needed to inline the full argument into \texttt{square\_subset\_square} directly, duplicating work each time a similar pattern arose.
 
\paragraph{Closing the target.}
By epoch $t\!\approx\!2600\,\mathrm{s}$, both direct dependencies of \texttt{SmallSquareInRectangle} were public, and the market was pricing its \texttt{PriceySatisfiedByAsset} contract accordingly. The strategy module directed $\alpha_2$ (newly freed after its own earlier publications) to this target. The final proof (Listing~\ref{lst:target}) makes two direct calls: \texttt{Complex.nhds\_hasBasis\_square p} at line 832, and \texttt{square\_subset\_square} at line 834. It was published at $t\!\approx\!2850\,\mathrm{s}$.

Several features of this trace illustrate the coordination mechanisms at work.
 
First, we observe parallelism on independent subchains. $\alpha_3$ worked on \texttt{Complex.nhds\_hasBasis\_square} (the hardest proof in the chain) concurrently with $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_2$'s progress up the \texttt{square\_subset\_square} branch. These two subchains share only the final target, so no publication events propagated between them until the last step. Epochs $t \in [1500, 2550]\,\mathrm{s}$ saw three agents doing fully independent, non-duplicative work.
 
Second, we see conjecture as task decomposition. Three of the seven declarations in the chain (\texttt{left\_mem\_rect}, \texttt{right\_mem\_rect}, \texttt{RectSubRect'}) did not appear in the original target set. They were introduced by agents mid-proof as auxiliary decompositions. Without a conjecture primitive, the work done by these three helpers would have had to be inlined into each of their users, substantially inflating the size (and difficulty) of the downstream proofs.
 
Third, publication-triggered cascades: The publication of \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff} at $t\!\approx\!2100\,\mathrm{s}$ immediately unblocked $\alpha_1$'s stalled attempt on \texttt{RectSubRect'}, and the publication of \texttt{RectSubRect'} at $t\!\approx\!2300\,\mathrm{s}$ immediately unblocked \texttt{square\_subset\_square}. Each publication was a dependency-unlock event that explicitly pushed towards the social optimum. One could observe settlement of the corresponding long contracts and a reweighting of open offers.


Fourth, the three agents took on qualitatively different work: $\alpha_2$ did bottom-of-chain scaffolding (the trivial helpers and their direct consumer, \texttt{rect\_subset\_iff}); $\alpha_1$ handled the utility-graph-middle work (\texttt{RectSubRect'} and \texttt{square\_subset\_square}); $\alpha_3$ handled the hardest independent proof (\texttt{Complex.nhds\_hasBasis\_square}).
