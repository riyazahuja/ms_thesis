\chapter{The Interdependent Task Allocation Problem}\label{chap:itap}

\subsection{Motivation}

The scheduling models of the previous chapter provide a rigorous language for
allocation under uncertainty, partial revelation, and precedence structure. However,
the research-allocation problems motivating this thesis are not fully captured by
classical scheduling alone. We now identify the ways in which mathematical development
departs from the standard scheduling paradigm, and use these departures to define a
generalization.

In classical scheduling, jobs are exogenous: the latent job set $\mathcal{J}^*$ is
fixed, and the scheduler allocates machines. Jobs may be revealed over time, but the
set of jobs that could in principle arise is determined before the process begins. In
mathematical development, this fails: proving a target theorem typically requires
intermediate results that did not exist before the work began. The act of completing
one job generates new jobs. Moreover, the planner can \emph{actively} create new jobs
by conjecturing --- proposing intermediate statements that, if true and provable,
would aid in the resolution of existing goals. The job set is not merely revealed over
time, but endogenously generated by the scheduler's own actions.

In classical scheduling, the processing requirement of a job is intrinsic, independent
of what other jobs have been completed. In mathematical development, this also fails:
the effective difficulty of proving a theorem depends on which lemmas, techniques, and
conceptual frameworks are already in place. Task difficulty is state-dependent.

Furthermore, mathematical jobs carry a fundamental uncertainty absent from classical
scheduling: the planner does not know whether a given statement is true or false. Each
statement $\varphi$ admits exactly two resolutions --- proving $\varphi$ or proving
$\neg\varphi$ --- and only one is achievable in finite time. An agent may spend
arbitrarily long attempting to prove a false statement; the only recourse is to
abandon the attempt and try the negation, or to work on other jobs that clarify which
direction is tractable.

Finally, since the centralized regime assumes no private information, it is natural
to allow multiple machines to collaborate on the same operation, pooling their
progress. This extends the transferable-progress convention of the scheduling chapter
to a setting where redundant assignment is permitted: multiple machines contribute
additively to a shared work counter, and the operation completes once the cumulative
total reaches the threshold.

These observations suggest a generalization of stochastic online scheduling in which:
\begin{itemize}
    \item each job corresponds to a mathematical statement with a hidden truth value,
    structured as a binary OR-job with two operations (prove or refute), only one of
    which has finite processing time;
    \item the job set grows endogenously, both passively (completing jobs reveals new
    jobs) and actively (the scheduler conjectures new intermediate statements);
    \item processing times depend on the current set of completed jobs, not merely on
    intrinsic job-machine parameters;
    \item multiple machines may work on the same operation simultaneously, pooling
    their contributions into a shared progress counter;
    \item and the scheduler acts under partial information about a latent environment
    encoding truth values, dependency structure, and machine capabilities.
\end{itemize}

We call this the \emph{Interdependent Task Allocation Problem} (ITAP) in its
centralized regime: all allocation decisions are made by a single scheduler. Agents
have no private information or strategic incentives; those features are introduced in
the decentralized regime of the following chapter.


\subsection{Formal Definition}

We define the centralized ITAP as a stochastic online scheduling instance with five
modifications. The resulting POMDP follows the same structure as the scheduling POMDP
of Definition~\ref{def:schedpomdp}; we specify only the points of departure.

\subsubsection{Binary OR-Job Structure}

In the scheduling POMDP, each job consists of one or more operations, all of which
must be completed for the job to be complete. In the centralized ITAP, jobs have a
different structure: each job corresponds to a mathematical statement and admits
exactly two alternative resolutions, only one of which is achievable.

\begin{definition}[Binary OR-job]\label{def:binaryorjob}
A \emph{binary OR-job} is a job $J_j$ with exactly two operations
$O_j = \{o_j^+, o_j^-\}$ and OR-completion semantics: $J_j$ is complete once
\emph{either} operation is complete. Formally, the completion family is
\[
W_j := \bigl\{\{o_j^+\},\; \{o_j^-\}\bigr\},
\]
and $J_j$ is complete at state $s$ if
$O_j \cap \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}(s) \neq \varnothing$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Relationship to classical shop environments]
The binary OR-job structure is not a special case of any classical shop environment.
In an open shop ($\alpha_1 = O$), each operation $O_{ij}$ must be processed on a
designated machine $M_i$, and \emph{all} operations must be completed. In a
parallel-machine environment ($\alpha_1 \in \{P, Q, R\}$), each job has a single
operation. The binary OR-job combines features of both: it has two operations (like a
shop), either of which suffices for completion (unlike a shop), and both operations
may be processed on any machine (unlike an open shop). This is a genuine extension
of the $\alpha \mid \beta \mid \gamma$ taxonomy.
\end{remark}

In the centralized ITAP, every job $J_j$ corresponds to a mathematical statement
$\varphi_j$ and is a binary OR-job: $o_j^+$ represents ``prove $\varphi_j$'' and
$o_j^-$ represents ``prove $\neg\varphi_j$.'' The job is \emph{resolved} once either
operation is completed.

The latent parameter $\theta(\omega)$ encodes a truth assignment
$T : \mathcal{J}^* \to \{+, -\}$ determining which operation is tractable:
\begin{itemize}
    \item if $T(j) = +$, then $o_j^+$ has finite processing time and $o_j^-$ is
    intractable ($\rho_{i,o_j^-} \equiv 0$ for all machines $M_i$);
    \item if $T(j) = -$, the roles are reversed.
\end{itemize}
The truth assignment is hidden from the scheduler.

We additionally require consistency with negation: for each $J_j \in \mathcal{J}^*$,
there exists a unique $\bar J_j \in \mathcal{J}^*$ with
$\varphi_{\bar j} = \neg\varphi_j$ and $T(\bar j) \neq T(j)$. Resolving $J_j$
automatically resolves $\bar J_j$ as well: formally,
$J_j \in \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s) \iff \bar J_j \in \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s)$.

\begin{remark}[Target set convention for negation pairs]
We adopt the convention that the target set $\mathcal{J}^{\mathrm{target}}$ is closed
under negation-pair identification: if $J_j \in \mathcal{J}^{\mathrm{target}}$, then
$\bar J_j \notin \mathcal{J}^{\mathrm{target}}$ (or equivalently, the target set
contains exactly one representative from each negation pair). This prevents
double-counting: resolving a single mathematical question counts as completing one
target job, not two.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{Transferable Progress with Redundant Assignment}

In the scheduling POMDP, each operation is assigned to at most one machine at a time
(single-processing constraint \textbf{(F3)}), and partial work transfers across
machines via the effective progress $Z(o; s) = \sum_i Y(o, M_i)/p_{io}(\omega)$. In
the centralized ITAP, we retain transferable progress but relax the single-processing
constraint: multiple machines may be simultaneously assigned to the same operation,
and their contributions are pooled additively.

Since the centralized regime assumes no private information or capabilities, it is
natural for all intermediate work to be freely shared. Each operation has normalized
total work content $1$, progress from different machines is additive, and the
operation completes once cumulative effective work reaches $1$.

Formally, the per-machine ledger $Y(o, M_i)$ of the scheduling POMDP is replaced by
a scalar \emph{latent effective work} variable
\[
W : \mathcal{O}^* \to [0, \infty),
\]
where $W(o)$ records the total effective work accrued on operation $o$ up to and
including time $t$. Operation $o$ is complete at state $s$ if and only if
$W(o) \ge 1$.

The one-step progress update for an operation $o$ under action $a$ is:
\[
W'(o) := W(o) + \sum_{i\,:\, a(M_i) = o}
\rho_{io}\bigl(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s),\, \theta(\omega)\bigr),
\]
where the sum ranges over all machines simultaneously assigned to $o$. If no machine
is assigned to $o$, the sum is zero and $W(o)$ is unchanged.

The scheduler does not directly observe $W(o)$ for incomplete operations, since the
realized rates $\rho_{io}$ depend on the hidden parameter $\theta(\omega)$. Instead,
the scheduler observes a separate \emph{cumulative assigned machine-steps} counter
\[
N : \mathcal{O}^* \to \mathbb{N}_0,
\]
where $N(o)$ records the total number of machine-time-steps that have been allocated
to operation $o$ up to time $t$, regardless of the effective work produced. The
counter $N$ is fully determined by the scheduler's own action history and is therefore
directly observable. The latent effective work $W(o)$ is inferred through the
posterior belief over $\theta$.

\begin{remark}[Redundancy as collaborative construction]
In the centralized regime, redundant assignment models collaborative work on a shared
task: multiple machines contribute to a common progress pool, each at their own
(possibly heterogeneous, state-dependent) rate. This is the natural convention when
there is no private information and all intermediate work is freely shared. By
contrast, in the decentralized regime introduced in the next chapter, agents maintain
private progress and do not pool work; redundant assignment in that setting models
independent parallel search, where the task completes when the first agent finishes.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Recovery of single-machine processing]
If one restricts to policies that assign at most one machine to each operation at any
time step, the sum reduces to a single term and the model recovers the standard
non-redundant scheduling semantics.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{State-Dependent Processing Times}

In the scheduling POMDP, the processing requirement $p_{io}(\omega)$ is an intrinsic
property of operation $o$ and machine $M_i$, independent of the state of the system.
In the centralized ITAP, the effective processing rate depends on the current set of
completed jobs. This models the phenomenon that completed lemmas make subsequent
proofs easier.

Formally, we replace the fixed processing rates $\rho_{io}(\omega) = 1/p_{io}(\omega)$
with state-dependent productivity maps:
\[
\rho_{io} : 2^{\mathcal{J}^*} \times \Theta \to [0, \infty),
\]
where $\rho_{io}(C, \vartheta)$ is the effective work rate of machine $M_i$ on
operation $o$ when the completed job set is $C \subseteq \mathcal{J}^*$ and the latent
environment is $\vartheta \in \Theta$. The one-step progress update for operation $o$
under action $a$ becomes:
\[
W'(o) := W(o) + \sum_{i\,:\, a(M_i) = o}
\rho_{io}\bigl(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s),\; \theta(\omega)\bigr),
\]
and completion is
$o \in \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}(s) \iff W(o) \ge 1$.

The truth-gating property is encoded via the productivity maps: if $T(j) = +$, then
$\rho_{i,o_j^-}(C, \vartheta) = 0$ for all $M_i$, all $C$, and all $\vartheta$ with
$T(j) = +$. The intractable direction accumulates no progress regardless of how many
machines are assigned to it.

The classical scheduling POMDP is recovered by taking $\rho_{io}(C, \vartheta) =
1/p_{io}(\omega)$ independent of $C$.

\subsubsection{Endogenous Revelation and Active Conjecturing}

In the scheduling POMDP, job revelation is governed by exogenous revelation times
$V_j(\omega)$. In the centralized ITAP, the visible job set grows through two
mechanisms.

\paragraph{Passive revelation.} A monotone revelation operator
\[
R : 2^{\mathcal{J}^*} \times \Theta \to 2^{\mathcal{J}^*}
\]
determines which jobs become visible as a function of the completed job set and the
latent environment, with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $R(C, \vartheta) \supseteq C$ for all $C \subseteq \mathcal{J}^*$;
    \item $C \subseteq C' \implies R(C, \vartheta) \subseteq R(C', \vartheta)$.
\end{itemize}
The exogenous-revelation model is recovered when $R(C, \vartheta) =
R(\varnothing, \vartheta)$ for all $C$.

\paragraph{Active revelation (conjecturing).} The scheduler may assign a machine to
\emph{conjecture} anchored at a visible job, actively proposing a new intermediate
statement. This is formalized by expanding the action space: in addition to the
standard work assignments $a(M_i) = o$ (assign machine $M_i$ to operation $o$), the
scheduler may select
\[
a(M_i) = \mathrm{Conj}(J_j)
\]
for a visible job $J_j$, meaning machine $M_i$ spends the time step conjecturing
anchored at $J_j$.

The outcome is governed by a conjecture kernel
\[
K^{\mathrm{conj}} : \mathcal{M} \times \mathcal{J}^* \times \mathbb{N}_0 \times
\Omega \;\to\; \mathcal{J}^* \cup \{\bot\},
\]
where $K^{\mathrm{conj}}(M_i, J_j, t, \omega) = J_k$ means a new job $J_k$ (and its
negation $\bar J_k$) is created, and $\bot$ means the attempt fails. Since
$K^{\mathrm{conj}}$ is a deterministic function of its arguments including $\omega$,
the latent-parameter POMDP structure is preserved: conjecture outcomes are encoded in
$\omega$ and are unknown to the scheduler in advance.

When a conjecture succeeds, the new job $J_k$ (together with $\bar J_k$) is added to
a conjectured set $D \subseteq \mathcal{J}^*$, which is maintained as a new component
of the state. The visible job set at state $s = (\omega, t, B, W, D)$ is then:
\[
\mathcal{J}_t(s) := R\bigl(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s),\,
\theta(\omega)\bigr) \cup D.
\]

\begin{remark}[Conjecturing as active task creation]
The conjecture kernel captures the creative act of proposing intermediate statements.
In mathematical practice, this is often guided by the current state of knowledge: an
agent working on a difficult theorem may propose a lemma that, if true, would simplify
the remaining argument. The anchor job $J_j$ formalizes this: the conjecture is
proposed in the context of work on $J_j$, and the quality and relevance of the result
depend on $J_j$ and the current state (via $\omega$).
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{Centralized ITAP: Full Definition}

We now assemble the modifications into a single definition.

\begin{definition}[Centralized ITAP instance]\label{def:citap}
A \textbf{centralized Interdependent Task Allocation Problem} (centralized ITAP)
instance is a stochastic online scheduling instance
\[
I = (\mathcal{J}^*,\, \mathcal{M},\, \alpha \mid \beta \mid \gamma,\,
(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P}),\, \mathcal{V})
\]
with the following specializations and modifications:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Binary OR-jobs.} Every job $J_j \in \mathcal{J}^*$ is a binary
    OR-job (Definition~\ref{def:binaryorjob}) with operations
    $O_j = \{o_j^+, o_j^-\}$ and OR-completion. A latent truth assignment
    $T : \mathcal{J}^* \to \{+,-\}$ is encoded in $\theta(\omega)$ such that the
    intractable operation of each job has $\rho \equiv 0$. Both operations may be
    processed on any machine.

    \item \textbf{Redundant transferable progress.} The single-processing
    constraint is relaxed (multiple machines may work on the same operation
    simultaneously). Progress is transferable and additive: cumulative effective
    work is tracked per operation via a scalar latent ledger
    $W(o) \in [0,\infty)$, and the operation completes when $W(o) \ge 1$.

    \item \textbf{State-dependent productivity.} Processing rates
    $\rho_{io}(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s), \theta(\omega))$ depend on the
    completed job set, replacing the fixed rates
    $\rho_{io}(\omega) = 1/p_{io}(\omega)$.

    \item \textbf{Endogenous revelation.} Exogenous revelation times $\mathcal{V}$
    are replaced by a monotone revelation operator
    $R : 2^{\mathcal{J}^*} \times \Theta \to 2^{\mathcal{J}^*}$.

    \item \textbf{Active conjecturing.} The action space is augmented with
    conjecture assignments $\mathrm{Conj}(J_j)$, governed by a conjecture kernel
    $K^{\mathrm{conj}} : \mathcal{M} \times \mathcal{J}^* \times \mathbb{N}_0
    \times \Omega \to \mathcal{J}^* \cup \{\bot\}$. The state is augmented with a
    conjectured set $D \subseteq \mathcal{J}^*$.
\end{enumerate}

The instance is additionally equipped with a finite target job set
$\mathcal{J}^{\mathrm{target}} \subseteq \mathcal{J}^*$ (containing exactly one
representative from each negation pair), a latent parameter space
$(\Theta, \mathcal{T})$ with $\theta : \Omega \to \Theta$, and an observation
convention $\mathcal{Z}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Centralized ITAP POMDP]\label{def:citappomdp}
The \textbf{centralized ITAP POMDP} induced by a centralized ITAP instance $I$ is
the stochastic shortest-path POMDP
\[
\mathcal{P}_I = (\mathsf{S}_I,\; \mathsf{A}_I,\; \mathsf{O}_I,\; T_I,\; T_I^0,\;
Z_I,\; c_I)
\]
defined by modifying the scheduling POMDP (Definition~\ref{def:schedpomdp}) as
follows.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{State.} A state is a tuple $s = (\omega, t, B, W, D)$, where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\omega \in \Omega$ is the latent scenario, drawn once at $t = 0$ and
    preserved by the transition kernel.
    \item $t \in \mathbb{N}_0$ is the current discrete time.
    \item $B : \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{O}^* \cup
    \{\mathrm{Conj}(J_j) : J_j \in \mathcal{J}^*\} \cup \{\varnothing\}$
    is the current assignment map.
    \item $W : \mathcal{O}^* \to [0, \infty)$ is the \emph{latent effective work
    ledger}, where $W(o)$ records the total effective work accrued on operation $o$
    up to and including time $t$.
    \item $D \subseteq \mathcal{J}^*$ is the conjectured set.
\end{itemize}

We define:
\[
\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}(s) := \{o \in \mathcal{O}^* : W(o) \ge 1\},
\qquad
\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s) := \{J_j \in \mathcal{J}^* :
O_j \cap \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}(s) \neq \varnothing\}.
\]
The visible job set is
$\mathcal{J}_t(s) := R(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s), \theta(\omega)) \cup D$.

Feasibility conditions from the scheduling POMDP are inherited with the following
modifications:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(F1)} (revelation) uses $\mathcal{J}_t(s)$ in place of
    $\mathcal{J}_t(\omega)$: if $J_j \notin \mathcal{J}_t(s)$, then $W(o) = 0$ for
    all $o \in O_j$, and $B(M_i) \neq o$ for all $M_i$.
    \item \textbf{(F2)} (machine routing) is replaced by the binary OR-job
    convention: both operations of each job may be processed on any machine, in any
    order.
    \item \textbf{(F3)} (single processing) is dropped: multiple machines may be
    assigned to the same operation.
    \item Feasibility additionally requires: $D$ is closed under negation
    ($J_j \in D \iff \bar J_j \in D$); if
    $J_j \in \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s)$, then $B(M_i) \notin O_j \cup
    \{\mathrm{Conj}(J_j)\}$ for all $M_i$; and conjecture assignments target only
    visible jobs.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Observation.} The observation is
\[
o = \bigl(\mathcal{J}_t,\; B,\; N|_{\mathcal{O}_t},\;
\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs}},\;
\Lambda^{\mathrm{prior}},\; \Lambda_t^{\mathrm{rev}},\;
\Lambda_t^{\mathrm{comp}},\; \Lambda_t^{\mathrm{conj}}\bigr),
\]
where the components are as follows.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$N|_{\mathcal{O}_t}$: Visible cumulative assigned machine-steps.}
For each visible operation $o \in \mathcal{O}_t$,
\[
N(o) := \sum_{t'=0}^{t} \bigl|\{M_i : a_{t'}(M_i) = o\}\bigr| \;\in\; \mathbb{N}_0
\]
records the total number of machine-time-steps that have been allocated to $o$ up to
time $t$. This quantity is fully determined by the scheduler's own action history and
is therefore directly observable. It is \emph{not} the latent effective work $W(o)$:
the relationship between $N(o)$ and $W(o)$ depends on the hidden rates
$\rho_{io}(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s_{t'}), \theta(\omega))$ at each past step.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs}}$: Observed completion sets.}
\[
\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,op}} := \{o \in \mathcal{O}_t :
\text{completion of } o \text{ has been revealed by time } t\},
\]
\[
\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,job}} := \{J_j :
O_j \cap \mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,op}} \neq \varnothing\}.
\]
As in the scheduling POMDP, completion is detected at the end of the time step in
which $W(o)$ first reaches $1$. Admissibility and policies are stated in terms of
$\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,op}}$ and $\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,job}}$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$\Lambda_t^{\mathrm{conj}}$: Cumulative conjecture record.}
For each past conjecture action, whether it succeeded ($J_k$) or failed ($\bot$).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$\Lambda^{\mathrm{prior}}$, $\Lambda_t^{\mathrm{rev}}$,
$\Lambda_t^{\mathrm{comp}}$} are defined as in the scheduling POMDP, with the
precedence sub-DAG replaced by whatever dependency information the observation
convention $\mathcal{Z}$ specifies.

\begin{remark}[Latent effective work vs.\ observable allocation]
The scheduler observes assignments ($B$), cumulative allocated machine-steps
($N|_{\mathcal{O}_t}$), completions ($\mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs}}$), revelation
metadata, completion metadata, and conjecture outcomes. The latent effective work
$W(o)$ for incomplete operations remains hidden: it depends on the realized rates
$\rho_{io}$, which involve the unobserved $\theta(\omega)$. The scheduler infers
$W(o)$ through the posterior $\Pi_t = \mathrm{Law}(\theta(\omega) \mid h_t)$.

In particular, if many machine-steps have been allocated to $o_j^+$ (i.e., $N(o_j^+)$
is large) without completion, this is Bayesian evidence that $\varphi_j$ is false:
since $\rho_{i,o_j^+} = 0$ when $T(j) = -$, large $N(o_j^+)$ with no completion
shifts the posterior toward $T(j) = -$. This inference mechanism is how the planner
learns about the hidden truth structure through interaction.
\end{remark}

The observation space $\mathsf{O}_I$ is the collection of all such tuples arising
from feasible states.

\paragraph{Actions.} An action is a map
\[
a : \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{O}^* \cup
\{\mathrm{Conj}(J_j) : J_j \in \mathcal{J}^*\} \cup \{\varnothing\}.
\]
The admissible action correspondence
$\mathcal{U}_I : \mathsf{O}_I \rightrightarrows \mathsf{A}_I$ requires:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{(A1)}] If $a(M_i) = o$ for $o \in O_j$: job $J_j$ is visible
    ($J_j \in \mathcal{J}_t$), not resolved
    ($J_j \notin \mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,job}}$), and $o$ is not complete
    ($o \notin \mathcal{C}_t^{\mathrm{obs,op}}$).

    \item[\textbf{(A2)}] If $a(M_i) = \mathrm{Conj}(J_j)$: job $J_j$ is visible
    ($J_j \in \mathcal{J}_t$).
\end{itemize}
Multiple machines may be assigned to the same operation or to conjecture anchored at
the same job. Every quantity referenced in \textbf{(A1)--(A2)} is visible in the
observation.

\paragraph{Transition.} The transition is $T_I(\cdot \mid s, a) :=
\delta_{\mathrm{Step}_I(s, a)}$, where
$\mathrm{Step}_I((\omega, t, B, W, D), a) = (\omega, t+1, a, W', D')$ with:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Progress update.} For each operation $o \in \mathcal{O}^*$:
\[
W'(o) := W(o) + \sum_{i\,:\, a(M_i) = o}
\rho_{io}\bigl(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s),\; \theta(\omega)\bigr).
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Conjecture update.} For each $M_i$ with
$a(M_i) = \mathrm{Conj}(J_j)$: if
$K^{\mathrm{conj}}(M_i, J_j, t, \omega) = J_k \neq \bot$, then
$D' := D \cup \{J_k, \bar J_k\}$. Otherwise $D$ is unchanged.

\paragraph{Initial state, cost, and policies.}

The initial state is
$s_0 = (\omega, 0, \varnothing, \mathbf{0}, \varnothing)$ with
$\omega \sim \mathbb{P}$. The terminal set is
\[
\mathsf{S}_I^{\mathrm{term}} := \{s \in \mathsf{S}_I :
\mathcal{J}^{\mathrm{target}} \subseteq \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{job}}(s)\}.
\]
The cost function and policy definitions are identical to those of
the scheduling POMDP (Definitions~\ref{def:randpolicy}--\ref{def:policyvalue}).

\begin{remark}[Conditional determinism]
All stochasticity is concentrated in $\omega \sim \mathbb{P}$. Conditional on
$\omega$, the process $(t, B_t, W_t, D_t)_{t \ge 0}$ is a deterministic dynamical
system, as in the scheduling POMDP. Truth values, processing rates, revelation
structure, and conjecture outcomes are all encoded in $\omega$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Recovery of the scheduling POMDP]
The scheduling POMDP of Definition~\ref{def:schedpomdp} is recovered by:
$K^{\mathrm{conj}} \equiv \bot$;
$R(C, \vartheta) = R(\varnothing, \vartheta)$ for all $C$;
$|O_j| = 1$ with AND-completion and deterministic truth ($T(j) = +$ for all $j$);
single-processing restored (at most one machine per operation);
$\rho_{io}(C, \vartheta) = 1/p_{io}(\omega)$ independent of $C$;
and the per-machine ledger $Y(o, M_i) \in \mathbb{N}_0$ of the scheduling POMDP
replacing the scalar latent-work ledger $W(o) \in [0, \infty)$.
\end{remark}
