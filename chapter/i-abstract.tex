Mathematical discovery is not a sequence of isolated proofs, but a distributed process across mathematicians and their expertise. We therefore model mathematical development as a scheduling and resource allocation problem, formalizing it as the Interdependent Task Allocation Problem (ITAP). Within a centralized-control regime, we study ITAP's complexity and approximation-theoretic structure. However, when agent capabilities are private, this regime becomes fragile, suggesting a decentralized formulation in which coordination is strategic rather than purely combinatorial is more tractible. We formalize this as a Verifiable Allocation Market POSG (VAMP) and show that exact equilibria computation is intractible in general, motivating a multi-agent reinforcement learning framework that approximates no-regret behavior in the empirical meta-game. We evaluate this model on synthetic ITAP environments against centralized baselines and apply the framework for theorem proving in Lean, showing that it improves multi-agent strategy amongst LLM baselines on research-level theorem proving tasks.
