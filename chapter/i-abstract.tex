Mathematical discovery is not a sequence of isolated proofs, but a distributed process across mathematicians and their expertise. Motivated by this picture, we formalize multi-agent theorem proving as an allocation problem over interdependent proof obligations. In the centralized regime, we model the problem as a POMDP, show that perfect public information is without loss of generality, establish NP-hardness and information-theoretic lower bounds, and give a $(2-1/m)$-approximate reactive scheduling algorithm. In the decentralized regime, we formulate the Multi-Agent Theorem Proving Game (MATPG), identify a publication externality, and introduce market mechanism that internalizes this externality, yielding bounded Price of Anarchy. 

Since exact equilibrium computation in the resulting market-mediated game is intractable, we develop a multi-agent reinforcement learning framework that converges to approximate coarse correlated equilibria, inheriting the efficiency guarantees of the market mechanism. We evaluate on synthetic environments, validating the theoretical claims, and instantiate the framework on real Lean~4 codebases via the Agora platform, showing that market-mediated coordination with a learned strategy module improves multi-agent proof completion on research-level formalization tasks.
