\chapter{Introduction}\label{chap:intro}




\subsection{Motivation}

In 1896, Jacques Hadamard and Charles Jean de la Vallée Poussin independently proved one of the central results of nineteenth-century mathematics: the number of primes less than $n$ grows asymptotically as $n/\ln n$. The Prime Number Theorem had been conjectured by Gauss and Legendre nearly a century earlier from numerical evidence, but its proof required mathematical tools entirely foreign at the time. Indeed, it took Riemann's study of the deep connection between the distribution of primes and the zeros of the zeta function --- combined with subsequent advances in contour integration, the theory of entire functions, and analytic continuation --- that ultimately made the proof possible. The PNT was not proved by a direct combinatorial or number theoretic attack on the primes, rather, it took centuries of collaborative development of shared infrastructure and techniques across disparate domains of mathematics before the pieces could be put together.

This pattern is the rule rather than the exception. Major mathematical breakthroughs almost never arise from isolated effort on a single problem. They emerge from the collaborative, cumulative construction of a shared intellectual infrastructure: definitions, lemmas, techniques, and conceptual frameworks developed by communities of researchers over years or decades.



This observation extends beyond pure mathematics. In any domain where progress depends on the accumulation of verified, interdependent results (e.g., software verification, scientific modeling, engineering design) the fundamental challenge is the same: how to allocate the efforts of many heterogeneous agents across a large pool of interdependent tasks, where completing one task may reveal or enable others, and where the ultimate goal is to resolve a designated set of targets as efficiently as possible.

The rapid maturation of formal verification systems (Lean, Rocq, Isabelle) and the emergence of large language models capable of generating formal proofs have made this challenge both more tractable and more urgent. Agentic AI systems are increasingly deployed to tackle large formalization efforts, and early results demonstrate that LLM-based provers can resolve nontrivial proof obligations at scale. However, existing systems overwhelmingly treat theorem proving as a search problem: each proof obligation is attacked independently, and coordination between agents (when multiple agents are used at all) is ad hoc. This leaves substantial efficiency gains on the table, particularly on projects with deep dependency structure, where the order in which results are established matters as much as whether any individual result can be proven.

This thesis reframes multi-agent theorem proving as an allocation problem over interdependent tasks, and develops the formal, game-theoretic, and algorithmic infrastructure to study it.

\subsection{Thesis Overview}

We begin by formalizing the problem of allocating heterogeneous agents across interdependent proof obligations, first in a centralized setting (a single planner directs all agents) and then in a decentralized setting (agents act independently with private information and individual incentives). We show that decentralization introduces a fundamental market failure and that market mechanisms can internalize this externality, yielding bounded-efficiency equilibria. We develop a multi-agent reinforcement learning framework for approximating these equilibria, and test the entire pipeline on a real Lean~4 codebase.

\paragraph{Chapter 2: Centralized Multi-Agent Theorem Proving.}
We introduce a formalism for mathematical tasks based on sequents, libraries, and dependency structures, and define the Centralized Multi-Agent Theorem Proving (MATP) problem as a POMDP. We show a reduction of this POMDP to a MDP, establish NP-hardness, and provide an algorithm that achieves a $(2-1/m)$-approximation to the optimal makespan with $m$ agents.

\paragraph{Chapter 3: Decentralized Multi-Agent Theorem Proving.}
We reformulate the problem as a non-cooperative POSG (the Multi-Agent Theorem Proving Game) in which each agent independently selects actions based on private observations and individual rewards. Under a canonical reward structure, we identify externalities and prove additional inefficiencies in this system. We resolve this by introducing a market mechanism: the Market-Mediated MATPG (MMATPG), and prove it has bounded price of anarchy.

\paragraph{Chapter 4: Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning.}
Motivated by the hardness of exact equilibrium computation, we introduce the coarse correlated equilibrium (CCE) as a tractable relaxation and show that no-regret learning dynamics converge to the CCE set. We develop a transformer-based MARL framework and prove it converges to an $\epsilon$-CCE of the full MMATPG.

\paragraph{Chapter 5: Experiments.}
We describe a synthetic implementation of the MMATPG, and present five experiments validating the theoretical claims: the RLS approximation ratio and lower-bound tightness, the publication externality and its market resolution, MARL convergence to approximate CCE with bounded PoA, ablation of framework components, and scaling behavior across instance sizes.

\paragraph{Chapter 6: Application to Neural Theorem Proving.}
We instantiate the MMATPG over real Lean~4 codebases and evaluate on the Prime Number Theorem formalization from the MiniCTX benchmark, comparing agentic theorem provers, MMATPG-scaffolded LLM agents, and LLM agents augmented with the MARL-trained strategy module.


\subsection{Contributions}

The main contributions of this thesis are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Formalism.} We introduce a sequent-and-library formalism for multi-agent theorem proving and define the centralized MATP as a POMDP, establishing PPI as a without-loss-of-generality relaxation.
    
    \item \textbf{Centralized complexity and algorithms.} We prove NP-hardness of the centralized makespan problem, establish work and serial-depth lower bounds, and give a $(2-1/m)$-approximate Reactive List Scheduling algorithm.
    
    \item \textbf{Game-theoretic formulation.} We define the MATPG (non-cooperative POSG), identify the publication externality, and prove that credit-based rewards yield unbounded Price of Anarchy.
    
    \item \textbf{Market mechanism.} We introduce the MMATPG (market-mediated MATPG) with collateralized bilateral contracts, prove that the market internalizes the publication externality, and establish a bounded PoA of $\frac{2-1/m}{1-\ell}$.
    
    \item \textbf{Learning framework.} We develop a MARL pipeline (decision transformer + MAPPO + PSRO) with convergence guarantees to approximate CCE, and show that the PoA bound extends to the learned equilibrium.
    
    \item \textbf{Synthetic evaluation.} We empirically validate these theoretical claims on synthetic instances and develop a training pipeline for the MMATPG problem.
    
    \item \textbf{Real-world instantiation.} We implement and test this market-mediated multi-agent theorem proving over real Lean~4 projects, against state-of-the-art agentic LLM provers.
\end{enumerate}
